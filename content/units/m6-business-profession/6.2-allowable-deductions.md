# Unit 6.2: Allowable Deductions (Sections 28-37)

## Overview

This is where the 2025 Act's reorganization of PGBP gets serious. The old deduction provisions (Sections 30-43B) have been restructured aggressively — sections merged, split, and renumbered throughout. Old Sections 30, 31, and 38 are now a single Section 28. Old Section 36 has been broken into four separate sections (29-32). And the MSME payment timeline under old Section 43B (now Section 37) got a substantive tightening that will hit every business dealing with MSME vendors. If you handle any business client, this unit demands careful attention.

---

## Old → New Mapping

| Old Section (1961 Act) | New Section (2025 Act) | Subject | Change Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| 30 (rent) + 31 (repairs) + 38 (building use) | **28** | Rent, repairs, insurance, and building maintenance | **Merged** + substantive change |
| 36(1)(i) — employee payments | **29** | Bonus, commission, and employer contributions to staff funds | Split from 36 |
| 36(1)(iv)/(iva)/(v) — insurance/pension | **30** | Employer contributions to recognized PF/pension, Keyman insurance | Split from 36 |
| 36(1)(vii)/(viia) — bad debts | **31** | Bad debts and provision for bad/doubtful debts (banks/FIs) | Split from 36 |
| 36(1) — other sub-clauses | **32** | Other deductions (family planning, animals, marked-to-market loss, banking cash transaction levy) | Split from 36 |
| 32 (depreciation) | **33** | Depreciation | Renumbered |
| 37 (general deduction) | **34** | General deduction (residuary) — expanded prohibited list | Renumbered + substantive change |
| 40 (amounts not deductible) | **35** | Amounts not deductible | Renumbered |
| 40A (expenses/payments not deductible) | **36** | Expenses not deductible beyond prescribed limits | Renumbered |
| 43B (actual payment basis) | **37** | Deductions allowable on actual payment | Renumbered + **substantive change (MSME)** |

## Section 28: Rent, Repairs, Insurance (Old Sections 30 + 31 + 38 — Merged)

### What Changed

Three standalone sections consolidated into one. The substance is mostly carried forward, but there is one notable codification:

**NEW — Proportionate deduction for partial business use.** If a building is used partly for business and partly for other purposes, only the proportionate amount attributable to business use is deductible. The old Act handled this through assessment practice and case law; the 2025 Act codifies it in the statute itself.

| Component | Old Section | New Section 28 | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rent for premises | 30(a) | 28 | Merged — no substantive change |
| Repairs and insurance — premises | 30(a) | 28 | Merged — no substantive change |
| Repairs and insurance — machinery/furniture | 31 | 28 | Merged — no substantive change |
| Proportionate deduction for partial business use | 38 (by assessment practice) | 28 | **Codified in statute** |

### Practical Impact

- No change to the deduction itself — rent, repairs, and insurance remain fully deductible for business premises and assets.
- The proportionate allocation for dual-use buildings is now statutory. If you were already doing this (you were), it simply formalizes existing practice. If a client was claiming full deduction for a dual-use building, that position is now explicitly barred by the Act.

**Bottom line: Same deductions, one section number instead of three. Update references. The proportionate-use rule is now black-letter law, not just assessment practice.**

## Sections 29-32: Old Section 36 — Split Into Four

Old Section 36 was a catch-all with over a dozen sub-clauses covering employee payments, insurance, bad debts, and miscellaneous items. The 2025 Act breaks it into four dedicated sections:

### Section 29 — Employee-Related Deductions (Old Section 36(1)(ii)/(iii))

Covers bonus, commission to employees, and employer contributions to recognized provident fund, approved superannuation fund, and approved gratuity fund. No substantive change — the breakout is purely organizational.

### Section 30 — Insurance and Pension Contributions (Old Section 36(1)(iv)/(iva)/(v))

Covers employer contributions to:
- Recognized provident fund and approved superannuation fund (subject to limits)
- Pension scheme under Section 80CCD (old Act) — now referenced under equivalent new Act provision
- Keyman insurance premium

No substantive change in deduction amounts or conditions.

### Section 31 — Bad Debts (Old Section 36(1)(vii)/(viia))

- Bad debts written off in accounts: deductible. Same conditions — must have been offered to tax as income in an earlier year or represent money lent in the ordinary course of banking/money-lending.
- Provision for bad/doubtful debts by scheduled banks, public financial institutions, and state financial corporations: deductible up to prescribed limits. Unchanged.

### Section 32 — Other Deductions (Old Section 36(1) — Remaining Sub-Clauses)

Residual bucket covering:
- Expenditure on family planning among employees (companies only)
- Loss of animals used for business/profession
- Marked-to-market loss or other expected loss as computed in prescribed manner
- Banking cash transaction levy

No substantive changes.

| Old Section 36 Sub-Clause | New Section | Subject | Change Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| 36(1)(ii)/(iii) | **29** | Employee bonus, commission, employer PF/gratuity contributions | Renumbered only |
| 36(1)(iv)/(iva)/(v) | **30** | Insurance premium, pension contributions | Renumbered only |
| 36(1)(vii)/(viia) | **31** | Bad debts and provision for doubtful debts | Renumbered only |
| 36(1) — remaining | **32** | Family planning, animal loss, MTM loss, banking levy | Renumbered only |

**Bottom line: Old Section 36 is now four sections (29-32). Same deductions, same conditions, same limits. The split makes each topic easier to locate — nothing else changed.**

## Section 33: Depreciation (Old Section 32)

### What Stayed the Same

- Block asset concept: retained
- WDV method: retained
- Depreciation rates: **unchanged** (rates prescribed in rules, not the Act)
- Additional depreciation for new plant and machinery in manufacturing: retained
- 180-day rule (half-year depreciation for assets used < 180 days): retained
- Unabsorbed depreciation carry-forward: retained (unlimited years)

### What Changed

**Goodwill excluded from depreciable intangible assets.** This formalizes the amendment made by the Taxation Laws (Amendment) Act 2021 and Finance Act 2021, which removed goodwill from the block of intangible assets eligible for depreciation. The 2025 Act carries this forward — goodwill is not a depreciable asset.

| Aspect | Old Act (Section 32) | New Act (Section 33) |
|---|---|---|
| Depreciation on tangible assets | Available | Available — unchanged |
| Depreciation on intangible assets (know-how, patents, copyrights, trademarks, licences, franchises) | Available | Available — unchanged |
| Depreciation on goodwill | **Not available** (post-2021 amendment) | **Not available** — codified in 2025 Act |
| Rates | As per Income Tax Rules | As per Income Tax Rules — **unchanged** |

> **Watch out:** If any client is still carrying goodwill in a depreciable asset block from pre-2021, the transition provisions govern how the WDV is computed. The 2025 Act does not reopen this — it simply confirms the post-2021 position. No new action required if you handled the 2021 transition correctly.

## Section 34: General Deduction (Old Section 37) — Expanded Prohibited List

### What Changed

Old Section 37(1) was the residuary deduction — any expenditure laid out wholly and exclusively for business/profession, not being capital or personal, and not covered by Sections 30-36. New Section 34 retains this principle but **expands the list of explicitly prohibited expenditures**.

| Prohibited Expenditure | Old Act (Section 37) | New Act (Section 34) |
|---|---|---|
| Expenditure for purpose which is an offence / prohibited by law | Prohibited (Explanation 1) | Prohibited — retained |
| CSR expenditure under Companies Act, 2013 | Prohibited (Explanation 2, added by Finance Act 2014) | Prohibited — retained |
| **Expenditure on illegal activities** | Covered implicitly under "offence" | **Explicitly listed** |
| **Expenditure on political advertisements** | Old Section 37(2B) (inserted 1978) already prohibited deduction for expenditure on advertisements in political party publications | **Retained and potentially broadened under Section 34** |

### Practical Impact

- The "wholly and exclusively for business" test remains the foundation. That has not changed.
- Political advertising was already prohibited under old Section 37(2B) (inserted 1978), which disallowed deductions for expenditure on advertisements in political party publications. The 2025 Act retains and potentially broadens this prohibition under Section 34.
- CSR remains non-deductible — no change from the Finance Act 2014 position.

**Bottom line: Same residuary deduction principle. Update the section number (37 → 34). Note the expanded prohibitions — political advertisements (already barred under old Section 37(2B)) are retained and potentially broadened, alongside CSR and illegal expenditure.**

---

## Section 35: Amounts Not Deductible (Old Section 40)

Renumbered. The provisions disallowing certain payments (interest/salary/commission to partners beyond prescribed limits, payments to non-residents without TDS, etc.) carry forward without substantive change.

## Section 36: Expenses Beyond Prescribed Limits (Old Section 40A)

Renumbered. The restrictions on cash payments exceeding Rs 10,000 (now Rs 10,000 per day per person), disallowance of excessive/unreasonable payments to relatives, etc. carry forward without substantive change.

## Section 37: Actual Payment Basis (Old Section 43B) — MSME Tightening

### This Is the Big One in This Unit

Old Section 43B allowed deductions for certain payments (tax, duty, cess, employer PF/ESI contributions, interest on loans from public FIs, leave encashment, etc.) only on actual payment basis — not on accrual. The principle carries forward, but the **MSME payment timeline has been substantively tightened**.

### What Changed — MSME Payments

Under old Section 43B (read with the MSMED Act 2006), payments to MSME suppliers had to be made within the time specified in the agreement (maximum 45 days, or 15 days if no agreement). If paid within this window, the deduction was allowed on accrual basis. The old Act had a further relaxation — payment by the due date of filing the return under Section 139(1) was sufficient.

**The filing-date grace period for MSME payments was removed by Finance Act 2023 (Section 43B(h), effective AY 2024-25). The 2025 Act carries forward this position in Section 37.**

| Aspect | Old Act (Section 43B) | New Act (Section 37) |
|---|---|---|
| General rule | Deduction on actual payment | Deduction on actual payment — same |
| MSME payment — with agreement | Pay within agreed period (max 45 days) | Pay within agreed period (max 45 days) — same timeline |
| MSME payment — no agreement | Pay within 15 days | Pay within 15 days — same timeline |
| Grace period (pay by return filing date) | **Available** for MSME payments | **NOT available** — must pay within 15/45 days, period |
| Consequence of late payment | Disallowance in the year of expense; allowed in year of actual payment | **Same consequence — but hits sooner** because no grace period |

### Practical Impact — This Will Catch People

Many businesses were relying on the Section 139(1) due date as a backstop — accrue the expense during the year, pay the MSME vendor before the return filing deadline (typically July/October), and claim the deduction. **That backstop is gone.**

If you have clients who:
- Purchase goods or services from MSME-registered suppliers
- Routinely pay after 45 days
- Were relying on the filing-date grace to claim deductions

They will face disallowance in the year the expense is incurred. The deduction will only be available in the year of actual payment.

**Action items:**
1. Review accounts payable aging for all MSME vendors across your client base
2. Ensure payment cycles are within 15 days (no agreement) or 45 days (with agreement)
3. Update client advisory templates to flag MSME payment discipline as a compliance priority
4. If clients are routinely paying MSME vendors late, model the disallowance impact on their tax liability

> **Watch out:** The definition of "micro and small enterprise" under the MSMED Act determines who qualifies as an MSME supplier. Your clients may not always know which vendors are MSME-registered. Advise them to collect MSME registration certificates (Udyam Registration) from all vendors as part of vendor onboarding. This was good practice before; it is now essential for avoiding disallowance.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Old Sections 30+31+38 merged into Section 28** (rent, repairs, insurance). Proportionate deduction for partial business use now codified.
- **Old Section 36 split into Sections 29-32** (employee payments, insurance, bad debts, miscellaneous). No substantive changes — organizational restructuring only.
- **Depreciation (32 → 33)**: Rates unchanged. Goodwill exclusion from intangibles confirmed (was already in effect post-2021).
- **General deduction (37 → 34)**: Expanded prohibited list — political advertisements now explicitly barred alongside CSR and illegal expenditure.
- **MSME payment rule (43B → 37)**: This is the change that matters most. No grace period — pay within 15/45 days or face disallowance. No backstop of paying by the return filing date. Review all client AP aging immediately.
- **Sections 35 and 36** (old 40 and 40A): Renumbered only. Same disallowance rules.
