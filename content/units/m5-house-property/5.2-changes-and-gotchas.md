# Unit 5.2: Changes and Gotchas

## Overview

The Income Tax Act, 2025 carries forward the house property computation framework largely intact. The charging section (old 22 becomes new 20), annual value determination (old 23 becomes new 21), and deductions (old 24 becomes new 22) follow the same logic. Standard deduction remains at 30%, the interest cap for self-occupied property stays at Rs 2,00,000, and the two self-occupied property limit introduced by Finance Act 2019 is retained. However, two substantive changes deserve close attention: the pre-construction interest cap is now explicitly stated as a combined limit, and the definition of self-occupied property has been liberalised. The loss set-off and carry-forward provisions are renumbered but unchanged in substance — though the interaction with the new tax regime creates a critical practice trap.

## Substantive Change 1: Pre-Construction Interest — Combined Cap Clarified (Section 22(2))

Under the 1961 Act, old Section 24(b) allowed deduction of interest on borrowed capital in five equal annual instalments for the period prior to completion of construction. A separate proviso capped total interest deduction at Rs 2,00,000 for self-occupied property. In practice, ambiguity arose as to whether the Rs 2,00,000 cap applied to current-year interest alone or to the aggregate of current-year interest plus the pre-construction interest instalment.

Section 22(2) of the 2025 Act resolves this. The Rs 2,00,000 is now explicitly a **combined cap** — the aggregate of current-year interest and the pre-construction interest instalment for that year must not exceed Rs 2,00,000 for self-occupied property.

**Practice implication:** Clients who were claiming current-year interest of Rs 2,00,000 *and* a separate pre-construction interest instalment on top of it are no longer on defensible ground. The combined limit is now express statutory text, not an interpretive position.

---

## Substantive Change 2: Self-Occupied Property — "Any Reason" Liberalisation (Section 21(2))

Under old Section 23(2)(a), a property was treated as self-occupied if it was in the occupation of the owner "for his own residence" and was not actually let out during the year. The condition was interpreted narrowly — the property had to be used for the owner's residential purposes.

This change was introduced by Finance Act 2025 as an amendment to old Section 23(2) of the 1961 Act, already operative from AY 2025-26. The 2025 Act carries it forward. Section 21(2) now provides that a property is treated as self-occupied if the owner "cannot occupy it for any reason" — a broader basis for non-occupation than the old Act's requirement of employment, business, or profession at another place.

**Who benefits:**

| Category | Effect |
|---|---|
| **NRIs** | A property in India left unoccupied while the owner resides abroad for employment can more clearly qualify as self-occupied |
| **Multi-city professionals** | An owner maintaining a property in one city while working from another city (not necessarily due to employer transfer) has stronger ground to claim self-occupied status |
| **Business owners** | Property unoccupied because the owner resides at the place of business qualifies — the old Act's focus on "employment" is now broadened to include business and profession |

## Loss from House Property — Set-Off and Carry Forward

The mechanics of house property loss set-off and carry forward are unchanged in substance. The section numbers have changed.

### Section Mapping

| Old Section | New Section | Provision |
|---|---|---|
| 71 | **109** | Inter-head set-off of losses |
| 71(3A) | **109(1)(b)** | Rs 2,00,000 cap on inter-head set-off of house property loss |
| 71B | **110** | Carry forward of unabsorbed house property loss — 8 assessment years |

### How It Works (Unchanged)

1. House property loss is first set off against income from other house properties (intra-head).
2. Any remaining loss is set off against income under other heads, subject to the Rs 2,00,000 cap per Section 109(1)(b).
3. Loss exceeding the Rs 2,00,000 inter-head cap is carried forward under Section 110 for set-off against house property income in the subsequent 8 assessment years.

---

## Critical Gotcha: New Tax Regime and House Property Loss

This is the single most consequential practice trap in the house property provisions.

**The rule:** Under the new tax regime (Section 202, which is the **default regime** from Tax Year 2026-27), house property loss **cannot be set off against income under other heads**. The Rs 2,00,000 inter-head set-off under Section 109(1)(b) is available **only under the old regime**.

**Why this matters:**

| Scenario | Old Regime | New Regime (Default) |
|---|---|---|
| Salary Rs 15,00,000 + HP loss Rs 2,50,000 | Set off Rs 2,00,000 against salary; carry forward Rs 50,000 | **No inter-head set-off**; entire Rs 2,50,000 carries forward for set-off against future HP income only |
| Net tax saving from HP loss set-off (30% bracket) | Rs 60,000 + cess | **Nil** |

Since the new regime is the **default**, clients do not receive the benefit of HP loss set-off unless they **actively opt for the old regime**. A client with significant housing loan interest who files under the default new regime without explicit opt-out will forfeit the inter-head set-off entirely.

**Action required:** For every client with a house property loss, evaluate whether the old regime produces a lower total tax liability after factoring in the HP loss set-off, Chapter VI-A deductions, and other benefits unavailable under the new regime.

## Transition Rules — Brought-Forward Losses

Sections 536(2)(m) and 536(2)(n) of the 2025 Act govern the transition of pre-existing losses.

| Rule | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Character preservation** | A house property loss brought forward from an assessment year under the 1961 Act retains its character as a house property loss under the 2025 Act |
| **Remaining period preserved** | If a loss was carried forward for 3 years under the old Act, it has 5 remaining years under the new Act — the 8-year clock does not restart |
| **Seamless transition** | No re-filing, no re-characterisation, no additional documentation required by statute |

**Practice note:** Despite the statutory seamlessness, working papers should document the original assessment year, old section basis (71B), and remaining carry-forward period for each brought-forward house property loss. When these losses are set off under the 2025 Act, the statutory reference changes from Section 71B to Section 110, and the basis documentation ensures audit trail continuity.

## Five Gotchas for Practice

### Gotcha 1: Pre-Construction Interest — Combined Cap Bites

Clients claiming current-year interest of Rs 1,80,000 and a pre-construction instalment of Rs 40,000 (total Rs 2,20,000) will find their deduction capped at Rs 2,00,000 under the now-explicit combined limit in Section 22(2). Review all ongoing construction-period loan files and recalculate deduction eligibility.

### Gotcha 2: New Regime Default — HP Loss Set-Off Unavailable

The new regime is the default. The inter-head set-off of house property loss is unavailable under it. Every client with a housing loan on a self-occupied or vacant property must be evaluated for old regime opt-in before the return is filed. Failing to opt actively results in loss of set-off benefit with no remedy post-filing.

### Gotcha 3: Section Numbering in Computation Sheets

All computation sheets, working papers, and client advisories must update section references. The most critical mappings:

| Old Reference | New Reference | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Section 24(a) | Section 22(1) | Standard deduction |
| Section 24(b) | Section 22(2) | Interest deduction |
| Section 23(2) | Section 21(2) | Self-occupied property |
| Section 71(3A) | Section 109(1)(b) | HP loss set-off cap |
| Section 71B | Section 110 | HP loss carry forward |

Using old section numbers in returns filed under the 2025 Act will create processing mismatches and potential notices.

### Gotcha 4: "Any Reason" for Self-Occupied — Broader Than It Looks

The liberalised self-occupied definition benefits NRIs and multi-city professionals. However, the broader language also means that the Department may scrutinise claims more carefully where the "reason" for non-occupation is not clearly linked to employment, business, or profession. Document the reason for non-occupation in working papers — a bare claim of self-occupied status without supporting facts is weaker under a broader provision than under a narrow one.

### Gotcha 5: Transition Documentation for Brought-Forward Losses

Brought-forward house property losses transition automatically under Section 536(2)(m) and (n). However, the old assessment order or intimation referencing Section 71B is the only documentary proof of the loss. Maintain a reconciliation schedule mapping each brought-forward loss to its original assessment year, old section, and remaining carry-forward period. This schedule becomes essential if the loss set-off is questioned during assessment under the new Act.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Two substantive changes** in house property provisions: the pre-construction interest combined cap (Section 22(2)) and the self-occupied "any reason" liberalisation (Section 21(2)). Everything else is renumbering.
- **The new regime trap** is the dominant practice risk — HP loss inter-head set-off is unavailable under the default regime. Active old-regime election is required.
- **Section renumbering** affects every computation document. Old Section 24 becomes new Section 22; old Section 71(3A) becomes new Section 109(1)(b); old Section 71B becomes new Section 110.
- **Transition is seamless** for brought-forward losses, but documentation discipline determines whether those losses survive scrutiny under the new Act.
- **Pre-construction interest** claims that previously straddled the cap boundary must be recalculated under the explicit combined limit.
