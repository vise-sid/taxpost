# Unit 9.6: Business-Specific Deductions (Sections 138-147)

## Overview

The 2025 Act carries forward a suite of profit-linked deductions for eligible business undertakings — Sections 80-IA through 80-LA of the 1961 Act — into Sections 138 through 147. Every one of these provisions is **transitional** in nature. No fresh claims are permitted under the 2025 Act. These are grandfather provisions that allow existing eligible undertakings to continue claiming the deduction for the remaining unexpired portion of their eligibility period, computed as if the 1961 Act had not been repealed. Section 122(5) (timely filing of the return) applies as a condition for claiming these deductions.

The key nuance — confirmed by the KarSetu FAQ (Q8.18) — is that while the deduction eligibility and computation rules are governed by the old Act provisions, the **quantum of business income** on which the deduction is applied is computed under the 2025 Act.

---

## Old → New Mapping

| Old Section (1961 Act) | New Section (2025 Act) | Subject | Change Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| 80-IA | **138** | Infrastructure facility, telecom, power — profit-linked deduction | Transitional grandfather |
| 80-IAB | **139** | SEZ developer — profit-linked deduction | Transitional grandfather |
| 80-IAC | **140** | Eligible startup — profit-linked deduction | Transitional grandfather |
| 80-IB | **141** | Industrial undertakings (various categories) — profit-linked deduction | Transitional grandfather |
| 80-IC | **142** | Undertakings in special category States (NE, Himachal, Uttarakhand, Sikkim) | Transitional grandfather |
| 80-IE | **143** | Undertakings in North-East States | Transitional grandfather |
| 80-JJAA | **145** | Additional employee cost — deduction for new employment | Transitional grandfather |
| 80-LA | **147** | Income of offshore banking unit or IFSC unit | Transitional grandfather |

---

## Nature of the Grandfather Provisions

### No Fresh Claims

None of these deductions are available for new undertakings set up on or after 1 April 2026 (the effective date of the 2025 Act). The 2025 Act's policy is to **not extend** profit-linked incentives to new ventures. The government's stated position is that the corporate tax rate reduction (to 22%/15%) substitutes for profit-linked deductions going forward.

### Existing Undertakings Continue

An undertaking that commenced operations before 1 April 2026 and was eligible for a deduction under the corresponding 1961 Act provision may continue to claim the deduction for the **remaining unexpired period** of the original eligibility window.

For example:
- A power plant that commenced generation in AY 2023-24 and was entitled to 15 years of deduction under Section 80-IA(4)(iv) has consumed 3 years (AY 2023-24, 2024-25, 2025-26). From AY 2026-27 onwards (under the 2025 Act), it claims the deduction under Section 138 for the remaining 12 years.
- A startup approved under Section 80-IAC before 31 March 2025 (for AY commencing before 2026-27) continues to claim for its 3-year eligibility window under Section 140.

### Computation: Old Rules, New Income Base

This is the critical nuance that practitioners must internalise:

| Aspect | Governed By |
|---|---|
| **Eligibility conditions** | Old Act provisions (as if the 1961 Act had not been repealed) |
| **Deduction period** | Old Act provisions (unexpired portion of original window) |
| **Deduction percentage** | Old Act provisions (100% or 30% or as specified) |
| **Business income on which deduction is computed** | **2025 Act** — business income computed under the new Act's provisions |

The KarSetu FAQ (Q8.18) clarifies: the deduction is computed on the profits and gains of the eligible business as derived under the 2025 Act, not as would have been computed under the 1961 Act. This means any differences in depreciation rates, expense disallowances, or income computation rules between the two Acts will flow through to the deduction quantum.

> **Practitioner alert:** Where the 2025 Act alters the computation of business income — through changes to depreciation schedules, expense recognition, or disallowance provisions — the deduction quantum under Sections 138-147 will differ from what would have been available under the old Act. The eligibility is frozen, but the base income is not.

## Section 138: Infrastructure, Telecom, Power (Old Section 80-IA)

### Categories Covered

| Sub-section | Activity | Original Eligibility Window |
|---|---|---|
| 80-IA(4)(i) | Infrastructure facility (roads, highways, water supply, ports, airports) | 15 consecutive years out of 20 from commencement |
| 80-IA(4)(ii) | Telecommunication services | 15 consecutive years out of 20 |
| 80-IA(4)(iii) | Industrial park or SEZ (pre-2006 approvals) | 15 consecutive years out of 20 |
| 80-IA(4)(iv) | Power generation, transmission, distribution | 15 consecutive years out of 20 |
| 80-IA(4)(v) | Renovation/modernisation of existing power distribution network | 15 consecutive years out of 20 |

### Deduction Rate

**100% of profits** for the first 5 years, **30% of profits** for the next 5 years (for most categories). Specific sub-sections may vary.

### Conditions Carried Forward

- Separate books of account for the eligible undertaking.
- Arm's-length transfer pricing for transactions between the eligible and other undertakings of the same assessee.
- No splitting or reconstruction of an existing business.
- Audit report in prescribed form.

**Transition:** An undertaking within its unexpired eligibility period as on 1 April 2026 continues to claim under Section 138. No fresh undertakings qualify.

---

## Section 139: SEZ Developer (Old Section 80-IAB)

### Eligibility

- **Who:** A developer of a Special Economic Zone, notified on or after 1 April 2005.
- **Deduction:** 100% of profits derived from the development of the SEZ for **10 consecutive years** out of 15 from the year of notification.

### Practical Status

Most SEZ developers' eligibility windows have largely expired or are nearing expiry. Very few new SEZ notifications occurred after 2015. Section 139 exists primarily for the residual tail of existing claims.

**Transition:** Unexpired period continues. No fresh SEZ developer deductions.

---

## Section 140: Eligible Startup (Old Section 80-IAC)

### Eligibility

- **Who:** An eligible startup incorporated between 1 April 2016 and 31 March 2025 (originally 31 March 2021, extended multiple times).
- **What:** Engaged in innovation, development, or improvement of products, processes, or services, or a scalable business model with high potential for employment generation or wealth creation.
- **Certification:** Must hold an Inter-Ministerial Board certificate.
- **Turnover cap:** Turnover should not exceed Rs 100 crore in the relevant previous year.

### Deduction

**100% of profits** for any **3 consecutive assessment years** out of 10 years from the year of incorporation.

### Conditions

- The startup should not be formed by splitting up or reconstruction of an existing business.
- Should not use plant and machinery previously used for any purpose (more than 20% of total value).

**Transition:** Startups incorporated before 31 March 2025 that have not exhausted their 3-year deduction window continue under Section 140. No startup incorporated on or after 1 April 2025 is eligible (the window has closed).

---

## Section 141: Industrial Undertakings — Various Categories (Old Section 80-IB)

Section 80-IB was an omnibus provision covering multiple categories of industrial undertakings with varying deduction rates and periods. Section 141 carries forward the residual claims for:

- Small-scale industrial undertakings
- Cold chain facilities
- Warehousing for agricultural produce
- Hotels (in specified areas)
- Hospitals (in specified areas — rural, excluding certain cities)
- Housing projects (approved before specified dates)
- Mineral oil and natural gas (exploration and production)

Most categories under Section 80-IB had sunset dates that have already passed. Section 141 services only undertakings within their unexpired eligibility window.

**No fresh claims permitted.**

---

## Section 142: Special Category States (Old Section 80-IC)

### Eligibility

- **Who:** Undertakings in Himachal Pradesh, Uttarakhand, Sikkim, or North-Eastern States that commenced operations between specified dates.
- **Deduction:** 100% of profits for the first 5 years, 25% (or 30% for certain categories) for the next 5 years — **10 years total**.

### Practical Status

The sunset date for commencement of operations under Section 80-IC was **1 April 2012** for most categories (extended to 1 April 2017 for some). Undertakings that commenced before the sunset and are within their 10-year window continue under Section 142.

**No fresh claims permitted.**

---

## Section 143: North-East States (Old Section 80-IE)

### Eligibility

- **Who:** Undertakings in the eight North-Eastern States (Arunachal Pradesh, Assam, Manipur, Meghalaya, Mizoram, Nagaland, Tripura, and Sikkim) that commenced operations between 1 April 2007 and 31 March 2017.
- **Deduction:** **100% of profits** for 10 consecutive assessment years.

### Practical Status

The latest possible commencement was 31 March 2017, giving a 10-year window expiring by AY 2027-28 at the latest. Section 143 services the final 1-2 years of claims for these undertakings.

**No fresh claims permitted.**

## Section 145: Additional Employee Cost (Old Section 80-JJAA)

### Eligibility

- **Who:** Any assessee whose business is subject to tax audit under Section 44AB (old Act) / Section 89 of the 2025 Act.
- **What:** Deduction for **30% of the additional employee cost** incurred in employing new employees.

### Conditions

| Condition | Detail |
|---|---|
| Minimum employment period | New employee must be employed for a minimum of **240 days** in the previous year (150 days for apparel/footwear/leather) |
| Salary threshold | Monthly emoluments of the new employee must not exceed **Rs 25,000** |
| Mode of payment | Salary must be paid by cheque, bank transfer, or electronic mode (not cash) |
| Deduction period | **3 consecutive assessment years** from the year in which the new employee is hired |

### Practical Significance

Unlike other provisions in this unit, Section 80-JJAA (now 145) was actively claimed by manufacturing and service companies creating new employment. The grandfather treatment means:

- Employees hired **before 1 April 2026** who are within the 3-year deduction window continue to generate the deduction under Section 145.
- Employees hired **on or after 1 April 2026** do not qualify — no fresh claims under the 2025 Act.

> **Practitioner tip:** For clients with significant new hiring in AY 2025-26, ensure the Section 80-JJAA claim is maximised in the last year under the 1961 Act. The 3-year tail will carry into the 2025 Act under Section 145 for those employees.

---

## Section 147: Offshore Banking / IFSC Units (Old Section 80-LA)

### Eligibility

- **Who:** A scheduled bank with an offshore banking unit in a Special Economic Zone, or a unit in an International Financial Services Centre (IFSC).
- **Deduction:** **100% of income** for the first 5 consecutive assessment years, and **50% of income** for the next 5 years — **10 years total**.

### Practical Status

With the IFSC Authority (IFSCA) now operational at GIFT City, Gujarat, existing units set up before 1 April 2026 continue to claim under Section 147. However, new IFSC incentives for units set up after 1 April 2026 are governed by separate IFSC-specific provisions, not Section 147.

**No fresh claims under Section 147 for units established on or after 1 April 2026.**

---

## Section 122(5): Timely Filing Requirement

All deductions under Sections 138-147 are subject to the timely return-filing requirement under **Section 122(5)** of the 2025 Act. If the return of income is not filed on or before the due date specified under Section 263(1), the deduction is **not available** for that assessment year.

This is a continuation of the requirement under the old Act (Section 80AC read with Section 139(1)), but practitioners should note the new section number.

> **Practitioner alert:** Missing the return-filing due date results in permanent loss of the deduction for that year — it cannot be claimed in a belated or revised return. For undertakings in the final years of their eligibility window, this is an irrecoverable loss.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **All transitional — no fresh claims:** Sections 138-147 are grandfather provisions. Only undertakings with unexpired eligibility windows as on 1 April 2026 may continue to claim.
- **Old rules, new income:** Eligibility and deduction period are per the 1961 Act provisions (as if unrepealed). But the business income on which the deduction is computed is derived under the 2025 Act's provisions (KarSetu FAQ Q8.18).
- **Section 122(5):** Timely filing of the return is mandatory for claiming any of these deductions.
- **Sections 80-IA → 138, 80-IAB → 139, 80-IAC → 140, 80-IB → 141, 80-IC → 142, 80-IE → 143, 80-JJAA → 145, 80-LA → 147.**
- **Policy shift:** The government has replaced profit-linked deductions with reduced corporate tax rates (22%/15%) for new undertakings. These sections are the tail end of the old incentive regime.
