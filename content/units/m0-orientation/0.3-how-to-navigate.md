# Unit 0.3: How to Navigate the New Act

## The Table-Based Format

The single most practically useful change in the 2025 Act is the introduction of **39 tables** that replace long prose descriptions. If you take away one thing from this unit, let it be this: **learn to read the tables**.

### Why Tables?

Under the old Act, determining something as routine as a TDS rate required reading through the full text of a section, including its provisos and explanations. The new Act presents the same information in structured rows and columns — scan to your row, read across.

### Key Tables You'll Use Daily

| Table Location | What It Contains | Old Act Equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| Section 393, Table 1 | TDS rates for payments to **residents** | Sections 194A, 194C, 194H, 194I, 194J, 194Q, etc. |
| Section 393, Table 2 | TDS rates for payments to **non-residents** | Sections 195, 196A, 196B, 196C, 196D |
| Section 393, Table 3 | TDS rates for payments by **any person** | Sections 194B, 194BB, 194LA, etc. |
| Section 58 | Presumptive taxation thresholds and rates | Sections 44AD, 44ADA, 44AE |
| Schedule VIII | Perquisite valuation rules | Section 17(2) + Rules |
| Schedule I | Tax rate tables | Finance Act schedules |

### How TDS Tables Work (Section 393)

The old way:
```
→ What payment type? → Find the right section (194A? 194C? 194J?)
→ Read the section text → Find the threshold → Find the rate
→ Check provisos → Check if the rate was amended by Finance Act
```

The new way:
```
→ Who is the recipient? → Resident (Table 1) / Non-resident (Table 2) / Any person (Table 3)
→ Find payment type row → Read threshold → Read rate
```

**Example — TDS on professional fees to a resident:**
- Old Act: Go to Section 194J → read through the section → find the rate → check provisos
- New Act: Section 393, Table 1 → find "Professional/technical fees" row → read rate and threshold

## Section 10's Transformation: 140 Clauses → Multiple Schedules

### The Old Section 10 Problem

Section 10 of the 1961 Act was the **most unwieldy section** in Indian tax law. It started with a simple purpose — listing income not included in total income — and grew into a monster:

- **140+ clauses** granting various exemptions
- Clauses for individuals, HUFs, NRIs, business trusts, IFSC units, specified entities, and more
- Finding the right exemption meant scrolling through 140+ clauses
- Each clause had its own provisos and explanations

### The New Architecture

The new Act uses **Section 11** as a gateway provision that references **Schedules II through VI** for exempt income. Additionally, **Schedule VII** lists persons exempt from tax, and **Schedule VIII** covers political parties and electoral trusts.

| Schedule | Category | What It Covers |
|---|---|---|
| Schedule II | General Exempt Income | Agricultural income, life insurance payouts, PF, scholarships, gallantry awards |
| Schedule III | Exempt Income of Eligible Persons | HUF member shares, partnership profit shares, disaster compensation, travel allowances |
| Schedule IV | Exempt Income of Non-Residents | NRE account interest, diplomat remuneration, foreign company exemptions |
| Schedule V | Business Trust & Investment Fund | InvIT/REIT distributions, investment fund income, unit holder exemptions |
| Schedule VI | IFSC Exemptions | International Financial Services Centre unit income |
| Schedule VII | Persons Exempt from Tax | Political parties, electoral trusts, educational institutions, government bodies |
| Schedule VIII | Political Party & Electoral Trust Income | Specific exempt income of political parties and electoral trusts |

**Note**: Section 11 specifically references Schedules II-VI for exempt *income*. Schedules VII-VIII deal with exempt *entities/persons* — a subtle but important distinction.

### How to Find an Exemption Now

**Old process:**
```
→ Go to Section 10
→ Scroll through 140+ clauses
→ Find the right clause number [10(10), 10(13A), 10(23C)...]
→ Read the clause + its provisos + its explanations
```

**New process:**
```
→ Ask: What type of person/entity?
→ Go to the relevant schedule (II for general, III for individual/HUF, etc.)
→ Find the exemption in a focused, shorter list
```

**Example — Gratuity exemption:**
- Old Act: Section 10(10) — clause 10 within Section 10 (yes, it was that confusing)
- New Act: Schedule III (Individual/HUF Exemptions) — clearly categorized

## Cross-Referencing Between Sections and Schedules

### The Cross-Reference Pattern

The new Act uses a consistent pattern: a **section** states the rule, and a **schedule** provides the details.

```
Section 11: "Income referred to in Schedules II to VI shall not be included in total income..."
    ↓
Schedule II: [lists general exempt incomes]
Schedule III: [lists eligible person exemptions]
...and so on through Schedule VI
```

This pattern repeats across the Act:
- **Section 17** (Salary/Perquisites) → **Rule 3 of Income Tax Rules** (Perquisite valuation tables)
- **Section 67** (Capital gains charging) → **Schedule I** (Tax rates)
- **Section 202** (New regime taxation) → **Schedule I** (Rate tables)

**Important**: Perquisite valuation remains in the **Income Tax Rules, 2026** (carried forward from Rule 3 of the 1962 Rules), not in a Schedule of the Act itself. The Rules contain internal tables for valuing accommodation, motor vehicles, and other perquisites.

### Reading Cross-References

When you encounter a section that says "as specified in Schedule X" or "in accordance with Table Y," the practice is:

1. Read the section for the **rule** (who, when, what conditions)
2. Go to the schedule/table for the **details** (amounts, rates, thresholds)

This is more readable than the old approach where everything was crammed into one section with provisos.

## The Parallel Reading Utility

### incometaxindia.gov.in Comparison Tool

The Income Tax Department has provided a **parallel reading utility** on its website that lets you:

1. **Search by old section number** → see the corresponding new section
2. **Search by new section number** → see the corresponding old section
3. **Compare text side-by-side** → see how the language changed

### How to Use It

1. Visit https://www.incometaxindia.gov.in
2. Navigate to the Income Tax Act 2025 section
3. Use the comparison/parallel reading feature
4. Enter either the old or new section number
5. View the mapped section with side-by-side text

This is your **primary reference tool** during the transition period. Bookmark it.

### Third-Party Mapping Tools

Several professional resources also provide section mapping:
- **EZTax** (eztax.in) — searchable section mapping table
- **ClearTax** (cleartax.in) — section comparison with explanations
- **Taxmann** — comprehensive commentary with old-new mapping

## Key Takeaways

- **39 tables** replace prose — learn to read them, especially TDS tables in Section 393
- **Section 10 → Section 11 + Schedules II-VII** — exemptions are now organized by person/entity type
- **Cross-reference pattern**: sections state rules, schedules/tables provide details
- **Parallel reading utility** on incometaxindia.gov.in is your primary transition tool
- **Focus on 20-30 high-frequency mappings** first — they cover 80% of daily practice
- **TDS trio**: 392 (salary), 393 (everything else), 394 (TCS)
- **No mathematical relationship** between old and new section numbers — learn through practice
