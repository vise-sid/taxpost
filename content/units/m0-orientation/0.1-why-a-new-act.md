# Unit 0.1: Why a New Act?

## The Problem with the 1961 Act

The Income Tax Act, 1961 was enacted when India had a GDP of roughly Rs 17,000 crore and a handful of taxpayers. Over 63 years, it was amended by every Finance Act, ordinance, and tribunal ruling until it became one of the most complex tax statutes in the world.

### The Numbers Tell the Story

| Metric | 1961 Act (as of 2024) |
|---|---|
| Total sections | 819 (originally ~298) |
| Total words | ~5.12 lakh |
| Provisos | ~1,200 |
| Explanations | ~900 |
| Chapters | 47 |
| Schedules | 14 |

### What Made It So Complex?

1. **Section number bloat**: New provisions were squeezed in using alphabetical suffixes — Section 80C, 80CC, 80CCA, 80CCB, 80CCC, 80CCD, 80CCE, 80CCF, 80CCG, 80CCH. Finding the right deduction required navigating a maze.

2. **Provisos and Explanations**: Instead of rewriting sections cleanly, Parliament added provisos (exceptions) and explanations (clarifications) on top of existing text. Some sections had 10+ provisos, each modifying behavior in a different scenario. Section 10 alone had 140+ clauses granting various exemptions.

3. **Redundant and dead provisions**: Several sections existed on paper but served no practical purpose. For example, Section 10A (relating to free trade zones) became redundant after FY 2012-13, yet remained in the statute.

4. **Scattered related provisions**: TDS alone was spread across 30+ sections (192, 193, 194, 194A, 194B, 194BB, 194C... up to 196D), each dealing with a different payment type. A professional handling TDS compliance had to cross-reference dozens of sections, thresholds, and rates.

5. **Archaic language**: Terms like "Previous Year" and "Assessment Year" confused even seasoned practitioners. A layperson hearing "Assessment Year 2025-26" would have no idea it referred to income earned in April 2024 to March 2025.

6. **Litigation magnet**: Ambiguous drafting led to enormous volumes of litigation. Conflicting tribunal and High Court judgments on identical provisions were common.

## The Simplification Mandate

### Budget 2024 Announcement

In her Union Budget speech on **July 23, 2024**, Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman announced that the government would undertake a **comprehensive review of the Income Tax Act, 1961** with the objective of making it:
- Concise
- Clear
- Easy to understand

The stated goal was to **reduce disputes, litigation, and compliance burden** for taxpayers. This was not about changing tax policy or rates — it was about rewriting the law in simpler language.

### What This Act is NOT

This is critical for CAs to understand upfront:

- **NOT a new tax policy**: No new taxes were introduced. No existing taxes were removed.
- **NOT a rate change exercise**: Tax rates, slabs, thresholds — all remain as they were under Finance Act 2024/2025 amendments.
- **NOT a policy overhaul**: Deductions under 80C (now Section 123), residential status rules, capital gains structure — the substance is the same.
- **It IS a rewrite**: Same law, clearer language, better organization, fewer pages.

Think of it as translating a 63-year-old book from legalese into plain English, reorganizing the chapters, removing duplicate pages, and adding a proper table of contents — without changing the story.

## The Select Committee Process

### How the Bill Became Law

The Income Tax Bill, 2025 went through a rigorous legislative process:

1. **Bill introduced in Lok Sabha**: February 13, 2025 — the original bill was tabled in Parliament.

2. **Referred to Select Committee**: Rather than rushing it through, the bill was referred to a **Select Committee of 31 members** from both houses of Parliament for detailed examination.

3. **285 recommendations received**: The Committee invited memoranda from stakeholders — professional bodies (ICAI, bar councils), industry associations, tax practitioners, and the public. A total of **285 recommendations** were submitted.

4. **84 substantive changes accepted**: Of the 285 recommendations, the Committee accepted **84 substantive amendments** to the bill. These were not cosmetic — they included clarifications on transition provisions, fixing of cross-references, and alignment of definitions.

5. **Original bill withdrawn**: The government withdrew the original bill introduced in February.

6. **Revised bill passed**: The revised bill incorporating the Committee's recommendations was introduced and **passed by both houses of Parliament** in the Budget Session 2025.

7. **Presidential assent**: The bill received the President's assent on **August 18, 2025**, becoming the **Income Tax Act, 2025**.

8. **Effective date**: **April 1, 2026** — applying to Tax Year 2026-27 onwards.

### Why the Committee Process Matters

The Select Committee process is significant because:
- It ensured the profession's voice was heard before enactment
- 84 substantive changes means the final Act is materially different from the first draft
- CAs who only read the original February 2025 bill need to re-read the enacted version
- The Committee's report is a valuable interpretive tool for understanding legislative intent

## What Changed in Numbers

| Metric | Old Act (1961) | New Act (2025) | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sections | 819 | 536 | -35% |
| Words | ~5.12 lakh | ~2.6 lakh | -49% |
| Chapters | 47 | 23 | -51% |
| Schedules | 14 | 16 | +2 |
| Provisos | ~1,200 | Eliminated | -100% |
| Explanations | ~900 | Converted to sub-sections | Restructured |
| Tables | Few | 39 new tables | +39 |
| Pages | 823 | 622 | -24% |

Key structural changes:
- **All provisos eliminated** — converted into standalone sub-sections or removed if redundant
- **Explanations converted** — now part of the main section text as numbered sub-sections
- **39 new tables** — complex provisions (TDS rates, perquisite values, presumptive thresholds) are now in tabular format for easy reference
- **Sequential numbering** — no more Section 80CCD(1B)(ii) — sections are numbered 1 through 536

## Why This Matters for CAs

As a Chartered Accountant, the new Act affects your practice in several immediate ways:

1. **New section numbers**: Every section reference you've memorized changes. Section 194C is now part of Section 393, Table 1. Section 80C is now Section 123. You need a new mental map.

2. **Client communication**: Clients will ask "what changed?" The answer is nuanced — the law didn't change, but the way it's written did. Being able to explain this clearly is a competitive advantage.

3. **Return filing**: From Tax Year 2026-27 (returns filed in July 2027), all forms, schedules, and references will use new section numbers.

4. **Transition period**: FY 2025-26 is the last year under the old Act. Any pending assessments, appeals, or proceedings initiated before April 1, 2026 continue under old section numbers. Managing both systems simultaneously is unavoidable.

5. **Opportunities**: CAs who master the new Act early can position themselves as go-to advisors during the transition. Most practitioners will struggle with the renumbering for the first 1-2 years.

---

## Key Takeaways

- The 1961 Act became unwieldy after 63 years of amendments — 819 sections, 5.12 lakh words, 1,200 provisos
- The 2025 Act is a **rewrite, not a reform** — same substance, clearer drafting
- No new taxes, no rate changes, no policy shifts
- Select Committee made 84 substantive changes to the original bill
- Effective April 1, 2026 — applies from Tax Year 2026-27
- CAs must learn new section numbers while managing the transition period
