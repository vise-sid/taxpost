# Unit 8.1: Income from Other Sources (Sections 92-95)

## Overview

The residuary head of income — "Income from Other Sources" — has been renumbered from Sections 56-59 of the 1961 Act to Sections 92-95 of the 2025 Act. The structural logic is preserved: a charging section with specific inclusions, a deductions section, a disallowance section, and a deemed-profits section. The substance is almost entirely carried forward. One notable improvement under the new tax regime is the **increase in the family pension deduction from Rs 15,000 to Rs 25,000**. The interest on savings accounts deduction has been unified under Section 153, merging old Sections 80TTA and 80TTB. Practitioners must be careful with Section references: **new Section 58 in the 2025 Act deals with presumptive taxation, NOT with amounts not deductible from other-sources income** (which is now Section 94).

---

## Old → New Mapping

| Old Section (1961 Act) | New Section (2025 Act) | Subject | Change Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| 56 | **92** | Charging section — income from other sources and specific inclusions | Renumbered; specific inclusions rearranged |
| 57 | **93** | Deductions from income from other sources | Renumbered; family pension deduction enhanced under new regime |
| 58 (amounts not deductible) | **94** | Amounts not deductible from income from other sources | Renumbered only |
| 59 | **95** | Deemed profits — remission or cessation of trading liability | Renumbered only |

> **WARNING — Section 58 Trap:** Under the 1961 Act, Section 58 dealt with amounts not deductible from other-sources income. Under the 2025 Act, **Section 58 deals with presumptive taxation** (old Sections 44AD/44ADA/44AE). The old Section 58 content has moved to **Section 94**. Practitioners citing Section 58 from muscle memory will land on a completely different provision.

---

## Section 92: Charging Section (Old Section 56)

Section 92(1) retains the residuary character: income of every kind that is not chargeable under any other head of income is taxable under this head, provided it is not entirely excluded from total income.

### Section 92(2): Specific Inclusions

Section 92(2) lists income items that are **always** taxable under this head, irrespective of whether they might otherwise fall elsewhere. The following inclusions correspond to old Section 56(2):

| Clause | Income Item | Old Provision | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 92(2)(a) | **Dividends** | 56(2)(i) | Classical system of taxation continues — dividends taxable in the hands of the recipient. This was introduced by Finance Act 2020 (abolishing DDT) and is carried forward, not a 2025 Act change. |
| 92(2)(b) | **Winnings from lotteries, crossword puzzles, races, card games, gambling, betting** | 56(2)(ib) | Taxable at special rates under Section 199(1). No change in treatment. |
| 92(2)(c) | **Employee contributions to staff welfare schemes** received by the employer | 56(2)(ic) | Continues the rule that belated crediting of employee PF/ESI contributions is taxable as employer's income. |
| 92(2)(d) | **Keyman insurance policy** proceeds where the policy was assigned | 56(2)(iv) | No substantive change. |
| 92(2)(e) | **Interest on securities** | 56(2)(id) | No substantive change. |
| 92(2)(f) | **Hire charges for machinery, plant, or furniture** let on hire (not being a business asset) | 56(2)(ii)/(iii) | No substantive change. |
| 92(2)(g) | **Forfeited advance** received for transfer of a capital asset | 56(2)(ix) | Continues the treatment introduced by Finance Act 2014 — the advance is taxable as other-sources income (and not deducted from cost of acquisition). |
| 92(2)(h) | **Compensation or other payment** on termination or modification of certain contracts | 56(2)(xi) | No substantive change. |
| 92(2)(i) | **Income from business trust** distributions | 56(2)(xii) | No substantive change. |
| 92(2)(j) | **Life insurance policy** proceeds not exempt under Section 10(10D) / new Section 15 | 56(2)(xiii) | Continues the post-2023 position where high-premium policies are taxable. |
| 92(2)(m) | **Gifts and inadequate consideration** (discussed in detail below) | 56(2)(x) | VDA explicitly included in the definition of "property." |

## Section 92(2)(m): Gifts and Inadequate Consideration (Old Section 56(2)(x))

This provision taxes receipts without consideration or for inadequate consideration. The architecture is unchanged:

### Money Received Without Consideration

- Aggregate value received from all persons during the tax year exceeding **Rs 50,000** is taxable in full (not just the excess).
- If aggregate is Rs 50,000 or below, the entire amount is exempt.

### Immovable Property — Without or Inadequate Consideration

- **Without consideration:** Taxable if stamp duty value exceeds Rs 50,000.
- **Inadequate consideration:** Taxable if stamp duty value exceeds consideration by more than **the higher of Rs 50,000 or 10% of the consideration**. The 10% tolerance band applies to immovable property only.

### Movable Property — Without or Inadequate Consideration

- **Without consideration:** Taxable if aggregate fair market value exceeds Rs 50,000.
- **Inadequate consideration:** Taxable if aggregate FMV exceeds consideration by more than Rs 50,000.

### VDA Inclusion

The definition of "property" for purposes of this section explicitly includes **Virtual Digital Assets (VDAs)**. This was introduced by Finance Act 2022 and is carried forward. It is not a new change under the 2025 Act.

### Exempted Recipients

The following categories continue to be excluded from the gift taxation provisions:

- Receipts from relatives (as defined)
- Receipts on the occasion of marriage
- Receipts under a will or by way of inheritance
- Receipts in contemplation of death of the payer
- Receipts from local authorities, registered trusts/institutions, hospitals, educational institutions, or funds specified in Section 92(2)(m) itself

## Section 93: Deductions (Old Section 57)

Section 93 allows deductions from income chargeable under the head "Income from Other Sources." The key deductions are:

### Revenue Expenditure

Any expenditure (not being capital expenditure) incurred wholly and exclusively for the purpose of earning such income is deductible.

### Family Pension Deduction

A deduction is available from family pension income equal to **one-third of the family pension or the applicable limit, whichever is lower.**

| Tax Regime | Deduction Limit | Old Limit (Section 57(iia)) |
|---|---|---|
| **New regime (default)** | **Rs 25,000** | Rs 15,000 |
| Old regime | Rs 15,000 | Rs 15,000 |

> **Delta: Family Pension Deduction Enhanced.** The increase from Rs 15,000 to Rs 25,000 applies **only under the new tax regime**. Under the old regime, the limit remains Rs 15,000. This is a genuine improvement for family pension recipients who opt for (or default into) the new regime.

### Section 93(2): Dividend Interest Expense — 20% Cap

Interest expenditure incurred in respect of dividend income is deductible, but capped at **20% of the dividend income**. This cap was introduced by Finance Act 2020 (simultaneously with the abolition of DDT) and is carried forward. It is not a 2025 Act change.

No other deduction is allowed against dividend income.

---

## Section 94: Amounts Not Deductible (Old Section 58)

Section 94 disallows the following from being claimed as deductions under Section 93:

- **Personal expenses** of the assessee
- **Interest or salary** paid outside India on which tax has not been deducted or paid (mirroring the disallowance under the business-income head)
- Any amount that is otherwise inadmissible

No substantive change from old Section 58.

---

## Section 95: Deemed Profits — Remission or Cessation of Liability (Old Section 59)

Where an assessee has obtained a deduction in respect of a loss, expenditure, or trading liability under the head "Income from Other Sources" in an earlier year, and subsequently obtains a **remission or cessation** of that liability, the amount remitted or ceased is deemed to be income of the year in which the remission or cessation occurs.

No substantive change from old Section 59.

---

## Virtual Digital Assets (VDAs) — Taxation Continues Unchanged

The taxation of VDAs at a **flat rate of 30%** (with no deduction for any expenditure other than cost of acquisition, and no set-off of losses) was introduced by Finance Act 2022. This regime continues unchanged under the 2025 Act:

- Section 199(2) imposes the 30% rate (corresponding to old Section 115BBH)
- No set-off of VDA losses against any other income
- No carry-forward of VDA losses
- TDS at 1% on VDA transfers continues (old Section 194S, now under Section 393)

These are **not** 2025 Act changes — they are existing provisions carried forward.

---

## Savings Bank Interest — Section 153 (Old Sections 80TTA + 80TTB)

The 2025 Act merges old Sections 80TTA (Rs 10,000 deduction for savings account interest for non-senior citizens) and 80TTB (Rs 50,000 deduction for deposit interest for senior citizens) into a single **Section 153**.

| Category | Deduction Limit | Old Section | New Section |
|---|---|---|---|
| Non-senior citizens — savings account interest | Rs 10,000 | 80TTA | **153** |
| Senior citizens — interest on deposits | Rs 50,000 | 80TTB | **153** |

> **Note:** The merger is a drafting consolidation. The limits and eligibility remain unchanged.

## Key Takeaways

| Topic | Old Position | New Position | Nature of Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Section mapping | 56-59 | 92-95 | Renumbered |
| Family pension deduction (new regime) | Rs 15,000 | **Rs 25,000** | Substantive improvement |
| Family pension deduction (old regime) | Rs 15,000 | Rs 15,000 | No change |
| Dividend interest cap at 20% | Finance Act 2020 | Carried forward | Not a 2025 Act change |
| VDA 30% flat rate | Finance Act 2022 | Carried forward | Not a 2025 Act change |
| VDA in "property" definition (gifts) | Finance Act 2022 | Carried forward | Not a 2025 Act change |
| 80TTA + 80TTB merger | Separate sections | Section 153 | Drafting consolidation |
| Section 58 content | Amounts not deductible | Now at Section 94 | **Renumbering trap** |
