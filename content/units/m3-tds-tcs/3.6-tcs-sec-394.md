# Unit 3.6: TCS (Section 394)

## Overview

Tax Collected at Source (TCS) under the Income Tax Act, 2025 is governed by **Section 394**, which consolidates all TCS provisions into a single table. The most significant development is not the structural consolidation — that mirrors what Section 393 did for TDS — but the **rate rationalization introduced by Finance Bill 2026**. Where the old Act had TCS rates ranging from 1% to 20% across different categories, the 2026 amendments have flattened most rates to a uniform **2%**. This is the biggest substantive change in the TCS framework in over a decade.

## Entry-by-Entry Analysis

### Sl. No. 1: Alcoholic Liquor (Old Rate 1% → New 2%)

TCS on sale of alcoholic liquor for human consumption. The seller of alcoholic liquor collects tax from the buyer at the point of sale. The rate has been **increased from 1% to 2%** as part of the uniform rate rationalization.

**Key point:** This applies to liquor sold for human consumption only. Industrial alcohol or denatured spirit does not fall within this entry.

### Sl. No. 2: Tendu Leaves (Old Rate 5% → New 2%)

TCS on sale of tendu leaves. The rate has been **reduced from 5% to 2%** — the most significant rate decrease in the table. Tendu leaves historically attracted a higher TCS rate due to their cash-intensive supply chain, but the 2026 rationalization brings them in line with other commodities.

### Sl. No. 3: Timber / Forest Produce (Old Rate 2.5% → New 2%)

TCS on sale of timber obtained under a forest lease, or any other forest produce (not being timber or tendu leaves). The rate moves from 2.5% to **2%** — a marginal reduction, aligning with the uniform rate.

### Sl. No. 4: Scrap (Old Rate 1% → New 2%)

TCS on sale of scrap. Rate increased from 1% to **2%**. "Scrap" means waste and scrap from manufacture or mechanical working of materials, not fit to be used as such without re-processing.

### Sl. No. 5: Coal / Lignite / Iron Ore (Old Rate 1% → New 2%)

TCS on sale of minerals being coal, lignite, and iron ore. Rate increased from 1% to **2%**. The seller of these minerals collects TCS from the buyer.

### Sl. No. 6: Motor Vehicle (Rate 1% — Unchanged)

TCS at **1%** on sale of a motor vehicle where the value exceeds **Rs 10 lakh**. This is the only goods-related entry where the rate has not been changed to 2%. The Rs 10 lakh threshold applies per transaction.

**Key point:** The motor vehicle TCS applies to the seller (typically a dealer). The threshold is the sale consideration, not the ex-showroom price. Vehicles below Rs 10 lakh remain outside TCS.

### Sl. No. 7(a): LRS Remittance — Education / Medical (Old Rate 5% → New 2%)

TCS at **2%** on remittances under the Liberalised Remittance Scheme for the purpose of education or medical treatment, where the aggregate remittance exceeds **Rs 10 lakh** in a financial year. The rate has been reduced from 5% to 2%.

**Key point:** The authorized dealer (bank or financial institution) processing the LRS remittance is responsible for collecting TCS. The Rs 10 lakh threshold is aggregate — once breached, TCS applies on the amount exceeding the threshold.

### Sl. No. 7(b): LRS Remittance — Other Purposes (Rate 20% — Unchanged)

TCS at **20%** on LRS remittances for purposes other than education or medical treatment, where the aggregate exceeds **Rs 10 lakh**. This rate remains unchanged — it is the highest TCS rate in the table and reflects the policy intent to discourage large foreign remittances for non-essential purposes.

**Key point:** The 20% rate applies on the amount exceeding Rs 10 lakh. For a remittance of Rs 15 lakh for investment abroad, TCS at 20% applies on Rs 5 lakh (the excess over the threshold).

### Sl. No. 8: Overseas Tour Programme Package (Old 5%/20% Tiered → New 2%)

TCS at **2%** on the sale of an overseas tour programme package. This entry has seen the most dramatic simplification:

| Old Structure | New Structure |
|---|---|
| 5% up to Rs 7 lakh | **2% — flat rate** |
| 20% above Rs 7 lakh | No tiered structure |
| Threshold of Rs 7 lakh applied | **Threshold removed entirely** |

Under the old Act, overseas tour packages had a two-tier rate with a Rs 7 lakh break point. Finance Bill 2026 replaces this with a flat 2% with **no threshold** — TCS applies from the first rupee.

**Key point:** The removal of the threshold means TCS is now collected on every overseas tour package, regardless of value. However, the rate reduction from 5%/20% to a flat 2% significantly reduces the overall TCS burden. The tour operator is the collector.

### Sl. No. 9: Parking Lot / Toll Plaza / Mining & Quarrying (Rate 2% — Unchanged)

TCS at **2%** on the grant of a lease or licence for parking lots, toll plazas, or the right to use land for mining and quarrying purposes (other than mining of minerals covered under Sl. Nos. 1–5). This rate was already at 2% and remains unchanged.

---

## Finance Bill 2026 Rate Rationalization — Summary

The overarching policy behind the 2026 amendments is **rate uniformity**. The old TCS regime had rates scattered across 1%, 2%, 2.5%, 5%, and 20%, with different entries at different rates for no apparent policy reason. The 2026 rationalization collapses most rates to 2%.

| Direction of Change | Entries | Old Rate → New Rate |
|---|---|---|
| **Increased to 2%** | Sl. 1 (alcoholic liquor), Sl. 4 (scrap), Sl. 5 (coal/lignite/iron ore) | 1% → 2% |
| **Reduced to 2%** | Sl. 2 (tendu leaves) | 5% → 2% |
| **Marginally reduced to 2%** | Sl. 3 (timber/forest) | 2.5% → 2% |
| **Reduced to 2%** | Sl. 7(a) (LRS education/medical) | 5% → 2% |
| **Simplified to flat 2%** | Sl. 8 (overseas tour) | 5%/20% tiered → 2% flat |
| **Unchanged** | Sl. 6 (motor vehicle) at 1%, Sl. 7(b) (LRS other) at 20%, Sl. 9 (parking/toll/mines) at 2% | — |

**Two outliers remain:** Motor vehicles at 1% (below the uniform rate) and LRS for non-education/non-medical purposes at 20% (a deliberate policy premium on discretionary foreign remittances).

---

## TCS Mechanism — How It Works

TCS operates in the reverse direction from TDS:

| Feature | TDS | TCS |
|---|---|---|
| Who deducts/collects | The payer (buyer of service) | The seller (provider of goods/service) |
| Direction of cash flow | Deducted from payment to payee | Collected from buyer in addition to sale price |
| Credit to | Payee (deductee) | Buyer (collectee) |

The seller adds TCS to the invoice or sale consideration, collects it from the buyer, and deposits it with the government. The buyer claims credit for TCS collected against their income tax liability.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Section 394 consolidates all TCS provisions** into a single table of 9 entries (with Sl. 7 split into 7(a) and 7(b)).
- **Finance Bill 2026 rationalized most TCS rates to a uniform 2%** — this is the single most important change for practitioners to internalize.
- **Rates increased** for alcoholic liquor (1% → 2%), scrap (1% → 2%), and coal/lignite/iron ore (1% → 2%).
- **Rates decreased** for tendu leaves (5% → 2%), LRS education/medical (5% → 2%), and overseas tour packages (5%/20% → flat 2%).
- **Two rates remain unchanged**: motor vehicle at 1% and LRS other purposes at 20%.
- **Overseas tour threshold removed** — TCS at 2% applies from the first rupee, but the rate reduction offsets the removal.
- **LRS Rs 10 lakh threshold** continues for both education/medical (Sl. 7(a)) and other purposes (Sl. 7(b)).
- **Motor vehicle Rs 10 lakh threshold** continues for Sl. 6.
- **Goods-based TCS** (Sl. 1–5, 9) has no monetary threshold — TCS applies on all sales regardless of value.
