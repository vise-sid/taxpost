# Unit 3.5: TDS — Any Person (Section 393, Table 3)

## Overview

Table 3 of Section 393 covers TDS provisions that apply to **"any person"** — meaning the deductor need not be a specified person, a company, or a government entity. The obligation falls on whoever makes the payment, regardless of the payer's status. This table contains seven entries covering lottery winnings, online gaming, horse racing, lottery commissions, large cash withdrawals, NSS/deposit withdrawals, and the newly introduced TDS on partner remuneration. The "any person" designation also means that these provisions apply regardless of whether the payee is a resident or non-resident.

## Complete Table 3: TDS by Any Person

| Sl. No. | Nature of Payment | Old Section | TDS Rate | Threshold |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Winnings from lottery, crossword puzzle, card game, or other game of any sort | 194B | **30%** | Rs 10,000 |

| 7 | Payment of salary, remuneration, commission, bonus, or interest by a firm to a partner | 194T | **10%** | Rs 20,000 |

## Entry-by-Entry Analysis

### Sl. No. 1: Lottery / Crossword / Card Game Winnings (Old 194B)

TDS at 30% applies when the aggregate winnings from lottery, crossword puzzle, card game, or any other game of any sort exceed Rs 10,000 during the financial year. The deductor is the person responsible for paying the winnings — typically the lottery distributor, game organizer, or platform operator.

**Key point:** The 30% rate is a flat rate. No deduction for expenses is permitted against such income. The threshold of Rs 10,000 applies to aggregate winnings from a single transaction or event, not cumulative winnings across the year.

### Sl. No. 2: Online Gaming Winnings (Old 194BA)

TDS at 30% applies on **net winnings** from online gaming. Unlike lottery winnings under Sl. No. 1, there is no fixed monetary threshold. Instead, TDS is triggered at the point of:
- Withdrawal from the user account, or
- At the end of the financial year

on the net winnings (i.e., aggregate deposits are netted against aggregate withdrawals and closing balance).

**Key point:** The net winnings concept was introduced specifically for online gaming to address the deposit-withdrawal cycle inherent in gaming platforms. The online gaming intermediary is responsible for deduction.

### Sl. No. 3: Horse Race Winnings (Old 194BB)

TDS at 30% on winnings from horse racing exceeding Rs 10,000. The bookmaker or the person responsible for paying the winnings is the deductor. The structure mirrors Sl. No. 1 — flat 30%, no expense deduction permitted.

### Sl. No. 4: Lottery Commission (Old 194G)

TDS at 2% on commission, remuneration, or prize paid on the sale of lottery tickets, where such payment exceeds Rs 20,000 during the financial year. The person organizing or conducting the lottery is the deductor.

**Distinction from Sl. No. 1:** Sl. No. 1 covers winnings received by the lottery winner. Sl. No. 4 covers commission received by the lottery ticket seller or agent. Different payees, different rates, different thresholds.

### Sl. No. 5: Cash Withdrawal (Old 194N)

TDS at 2% on cash withdrawals exceeding **Rs 1 crore** in aggregate during the financial year from one or more accounts maintained with a banking company, co-operative bank, or post office.

**Co-operative society exception:** For a co-operative society, the threshold is elevated to **Rs 3 crore**. This higher threshold recognizes the cash-intensive nature of rural co-operative transactions.

**Simplification:** The old Section 194N contained a higher TDS rate (5%) for non-filers of returns, triggered through the Section 206AB mechanism. Sections 206AB and 206CCA were first removed from the 1961 Act by Finance Act 2025 (effective 1 April 2025). The 2025 Act does not re-enact them. The uniform 2% rate now applies to all payees regardless of return-filing status.

**Key point:** The purpose of Section 194N is not revenue collection — it is a deterrent against excessive cash transactions. The TDS is fully adjustable against the payee's tax liability.

### Sl. No. 6: NSS / Deposits (Old 194EE)

TDS at 10% on payments in respect of deposits under the National Savings Scheme, or deposits referred to in Section 80CCA of the old Act, where such payment exceeds **Rs 2,500** during the financial year.

**Key point:** This provision is of diminishing practical relevance since NSS deposits were frozen for new subscriptions after November 1992. It continues to apply to withdrawals from legacy accounts.

### Sl. No. 7: Partner Salary / Remuneration / Interest (Old 194T — New, 2024)

TDS at 10% on salary, remuneration, commission, bonus, or interest paid by a **firm to its partner**, where the aggregate amount exceeds **Rs 20,000** during the financial year.

**This is the newest entry in the TDS framework.** Section 194T was introduced by the Finance (No. 2) Act, 2024, effective from 1 April 2025. It closes a long-standing gap — previously, payments by firms to partners were outside the TDS net entirely.

**What is covered:**
- Salary or remuneration paid under the partnership deed
- Commission or bonus paid to partners
- Interest on capital or any other interest credited or paid to partners

**What this means in practice:** Every firm — whether a traditional partnership or an LLP — must now deduct TDS at 10% on partner payments exceeding Rs 20,000 in aggregate. The deduction obligation arises at the earlier of credit to the partner's capital account or actual payment.

**Key point:** The Rs 20,000 threshold is aggregate across all payment types (salary + interest + commission). If a partner receives Rs 15,000 as salary and Rs 10,000 as interest, the aggregate of Rs 25,000 exceeds the threshold, and TDS applies on the entire amount.

## "Any Person" — What It Means

The classification "any person" in Table 3 carries a specific legal meaning:

| Deductor Classification | Tables | Who Must Deduct |
|---|---|---|
| Specified person (as defined in Section 393) | Table 1 | Only entities meeting the "specified person" definition |
| Any person | Table 2 (non-resident payments), Table 3 | **Every person** making the specified payment |

For Table 3 entries, the deduction obligation falls on whoever makes the payment. An individual paying lottery winnings, a sole proprietor operating a horse racing establishment, a small partnership firm paying partner salary — all are required to deduct TDS regardless of their turnover, audit status, or entity type.

**Resident / non-resident status of the payee is also irrelevant.** Unlike Table 1 (resident-specific) and Table 2 (non-resident-specific), Table 3 applies to payments made to any payee.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Table 3 contains 7 entries** — all requiring deduction by "any person," regardless of the deductor's status or the payee's residential status.
- **Three entries at 30%**: lottery/crossword/card game winnings (Sl. 1), online gaming (Sl. 2), and horse racing (Sl. 3) — these cover speculative and gaming income.
- **Two entries at 2%**: lottery commission (Sl. 4) and cash withdrawal (Sl. 5) — lower rates for commercial/deterrent purposes.
- **Two entries at 10%**: NSS/deposits (Sl. 6) and partner salary/remuneration/interest (Sl. 7).
- **194N simplified**: the higher rate for non-filers (old 206AB mechanism) has been dropped; a uniform 2% rate applies to all cash withdrawals above threshold.
- **194T is the newest provision**: introduced in 2024, it brings firm-to-partner payments into the TDS net for the first time, with a Rs 20,000 aggregate threshold.
- **Online gaming (Sl. 2) has no fixed monetary threshold** — TDS is computed on net winnings at the point of withdrawal or year-end.
- **Co-operative societies get a Rs 3 crore threshold** under Sl. 5, versus Rs 1 crore for all other payees.
