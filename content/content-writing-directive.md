# Content Writing Directive — Taxpost Unit Files

## The One Rule That Governs Everything

**Our users are Chartered Accountants. They already know income tax law. They do NOT need us to teach them what TDS is, how capital gains work, or why residential status matters.**

**What they need is: what changed, where it moved, and what to do differently starting April 1, 2026.**

Every sentence in every unit file must pass this test:

> "Does this tell a CA something they don't already know from 5-30 years of practice?"

If the answer is no, delete it.

---

## The Guidebook Perspective

These unit files aren't just backend source material — they're rendered directly in the app as a **Guidebook** that CAs can open and read alongside their lessons. This changes how we write.

### Who Is Reading This, and When?

Picture the reader: a CA sitting in their office at 9 PM after a long day of client work. They just got 3 out of 5 questions wrong on a TDS lesson. They tap "Open Guidebook" because they want to understand the new TDS structure properly before retrying.

Or: a CA in a client meeting, phone under the table, quickly scanning the Guidebook to confirm whether Section 194J maps to 393 Table 1 or Table 2 before giving advice.

Or: a CA on a Sunday morning, coffee in hand, reading through the Orientation module because they want to feel prepared before April 1.

**Three modes of use:**
1. **Study mode** — reading end-to-end to build understanding (Sunday morning)
2. **Recovery mode** — reading after failing questions to fill gaps (9 PM at office)
3. **Reference mode** — scanning for a specific fact mid-work (client meeting)

The content must work for all three.

### How It Should Feel to the Reader

**Like a senior colleague's personal notes — not a textbook, not a government circular.**

Imagine a CA with 20 years of experience spent their weekend studying the new Act, made detailed notes of everything that matters, and shared those notes with you. That's the voice. That's the level. That's the format.

- It assumes you're competent. It doesn't explain basics.
- It highlights what surprised them. What tripped them up. What they almost missed.
- It's organized for quick access, not for sequential reading.
- It has opinions: "This is the change that'll catch most people" / "This one's just renumbering, don't overthink it."
- It respects your time — no filler, no padding, no word count targets.

### The Guidebook Should Make the CA Feel...

- **Informed**, not lectured — "I now know exactly what changed"
- **Confident**, not anxious — "I can handle April 1"
- **Respected**, not patronized — "This was written for someone at my level"
- **Efficient**, not overwhelmed — "I found what I needed in 30 seconds"

### Guidebook-Specific Writing Guidelines

1. **Open with "Why This Unit Matters"** — 2 lines max. Connect to daily practice. "Starting April 1, every TDS certificate your firm issues needs new section numbers. This unit covers exactly which numbers change."

2. **Use "you" freely** — "You'll cite Section 392 where you used to cite 192." Direct address keeps it personal and practical.

3. **Add context a textbook wouldn't** — "This is one of the few sections where the substance actually changed, not just the number. Pay attention." A senior colleague would say this. A textbook wouldn't.

4. **Make it skimmable on a phone** — Most CAs will read this on mobile. Short paragraphs. Bold key terms. Tables that fit a small screen. No wall of text.

5. **End sections with a one-liner** — After each topic block, a single bold sentence summarizing the takeaway. "**Bottom line: Same deduction, new number. Update your templates, nothing else.**"

6. **Don't repeat what the questions already test** — The Guidebook and the questions are complementary, not redundant. The Guidebook explains, the questions verify. If a fact is tested in a question, the Guidebook should provide the context and reasoning behind that fact, not just restate it.

## The Perspective: Delta, Not Tutorial

Think of our content like a **migration guide**, not a textbook.

When a software library releases v2, the documentation doesn't re-explain what a database is. It says: "Connection pooling moved from `config.pool` to `db.connections`. The default pool size changed from 5 to 10. Here's what your code should look like now."

That's exactly what our content does — but for tax law.

### What We ARE
- A **section mapping guide**: old number → new number, with context on what (if anything) changed substantively
- A **delta document**: what's different, what's the same, what's gone
- A **practice transition manual**: what CAs need to do differently in their daily work
- A **quick-reference resource**: structured so a CA can look up a specific change in 30 seconds

### What We Are NOT
- A tax textbook
- A beginner's guide to income tax
- A CA exam preparation resource
- A commentary or treatise on the law

## Writing Rules

### Rule 1: Never Explain What the CA Already Knows

**Wrong:**
> "TDS (Tax Deducted at Source) is a mechanism where the payer deducts tax at the time of payment and deposits it with the government. It ensures regular collection of tax revenue and reduces the burden on the taxpayer at the time of filing returns."

A CA has been handling TDS for years. This is insulting.

**Right:**
> "TDS was spread across 30+ sections (192-196D) in the old Act. The new Act consolidates everything into three sections: 392 (salary), 393 (all other TDS, organized in three tables), and 394 (TCS)."

This tells them something new in 2 sentences.

### Rule 2: Lead With the Mapping

For every provision covered, the FIRST thing the CA wants to know is: **old section → new section**. Lead with this. Always.

**Structure every provision as:**
1. Old section number → New section number
2. What changed substantively (if anything)
3. What stayed the same (important — CAs need to know what NOT to relearn)
4. Practical impact on daily work
5. Gotchas or common mistakes during transition

### Rule 3: Distinguish "Moved" from "Changed"

Most provisions were **renumbered** (moved) without substantive change. Some were **actually changed**. CAs need to know the difference clearly.

Use explicit labels:
- **Renumbered only** — same substance, new number. Just update your references.
- **Restructured** — same substance, but organized differently (e.g., prose → table, section → schedule). Understand the new format.
- **Substantively changed** — the actual rule changed. Study carefully.
- **Removed** — provision no longer exists. Know why.
- **New** — didn't exist before. Learn from scratch.

### Rule 4: Use Tables Over Prose

CAs are practitioners, not readers. They want to scan, not study.

**Wrong:** A paragraph explaining that Section 194C is now in Section 393 Table 1, and Section 194H is also in Section 393 Table 1, and Section 194I is also in Table 1 but with an expanded definition...

**Right:**

| Old Section | Payment Type | New Location | What Changed |
|---|---|---|---|
| 194C | Contractor payments | 393, Table 1 | Renumbered only |
| 194H | Commission/brokerage | 393, Table 1 | Renumbered only |
| 194-I | Rent | 393, Table 1 | Definition expanded: factory buildings now included |

### Rule 5: Highlight What the CA Must DO Differently

Theory is useless without action. Every section should answer: **"So what do I do differently on Monday morning?"**

Examples of actionable content:
- "Update all TDS certificate templates to cite Section 392 instead of Section 192"
- "Verify that your tax software maps old 194J to new 393, Table 1, S.No. 6(iii)"
- "Inform clients that their 80C investments now fall under Section 123 — same benefit, new reference"

### Rule 6: Flag the Traps

Where will CAs make mistakes? Call these out explicitly.

**Format:**
> **Watch out:** The old Act used "for the purpose of employment outside India" — the new Act says "for employment outside India." The deletion of "for the purpose of" could be interpreted to narrow the scope. Monitor CBDT clarifications on this.

### Rule 7: Keep It Scannable

- Use headers liberally (H2 for major sections, H3 for sub-topics)
- Use tables for any mapping or comparison
- Use bold for key terms and section numbers
- Use bullet lists, not paragraphs, for multi-point information
- Maximum 3-4 lines per paragraph
- Include a "Key Takeaways" section at the end of every unit

### Rule 8: Cite Exact Section Numbers — Both Old and New

Every reference should include both the old and new section number so the CA can cross-reference. Format: **old Section X → new Section Y**.

Never reference a section without its number. "The provision on salary TDS" is vague. "Section 192 → Section 392" is precise.

### Rule 9: Sources Must Be Verifiable

End every unit file with a ### Sources section containing exact URLs. No vague attributions like "as per government sources" or "according to experts."

### Rule 10: Write for the Guidebook

These unit files serve dual purpose:
1. **Source material** for generating questions (internal)
2. **User-facing Guidebook** content (rendered in the app for CAs who want to read)

So the content must be:
- Accurate enough to generate precise questions from
- Readable enough that a CA finds it useful as a reference
- Structured enough that a CA can find what they need without reading the whole file

## Tone

- **Peer-to-peer**: Write as one CA to another. Not teacher-to-student.
- **Confident**: State facts directly. No hedging ("it appears that..." / "it seems like...").
- **Practical**: Every paragraph should feel useful, not academic.
- **Respectful of expertise**: The reader has more domain experience than the writer. Never condescend.

## Unit File Template

```markdown
# Unit X.Y: [Title]

## Overview
[2-3 sentences: what this unit covers and why it matters for daily practice]

## [Topic Section — repeat as needed]

### Old → New Mapping
[Table or concise mapping]

### What Changed
[Only substantive changes — skip if renumbered only]

### What Stayed the Same
[Reassure CAs on what they DON'T need to relearn]

### Practical Impact
[What to do differently in practice]

### Watch Out
[Traps, common mistakes, ambiguities to monitor]

## Key Takeaways
[5-7 bullet points summarizing the unit]

---

### Sources
[Exact URLs]
```

## The Litmus Test

Before finalizing any unit file, ask:

1. **Would a CA with 10 years of experience learn something new from every section?** If not, cut the fluff.
2. **Could a CA use this as a quick-reference during work?** If not, restructure.
3. **Does every claim have a section number attached?** If not, add it.
4. **Is the delta clear?** Can the CA tell in 5 seconds whether a provision moved, changed, or was removed?
5. **Are the questions generatable?** Is there enough precise, factual content to create Tier 1/2/3 questions from?
